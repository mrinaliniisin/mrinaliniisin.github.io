The plot, walked through

Opening — Jerusalem

Poirot, on holiday, overhears Raymond saying to Carol: "You do see, don't you, that she's got to be killed?" He files it away.

He encounters the Boyntons, the doctors (Sarah, Gerard), Westholme, Cope, and the nun. Sarah and Raymond fall in love. Sarah, appalled by Lady Boynton's cruelty, confronts her — and Lady Boynton fires back a strange threat: "I never forget. Not a face, not a name." Sarah thinks it's directed at her. It isn't. It's aimed at someone standing behind her — Westholme.

Analogy: Lady Boynton is a sniper firing a single warning shot. You only realise later who was actually in her crosshairs.

The party moves to the dig

Lord Boynton excavates for John the Baptist's skull, with Leonard helping. Lady Boynton perches on a high outcrop ("the Machaan") overlooking the dig. From there she can see everyone. Or rather — everyone can see her, sitting motionless, throughout the afternoon.

The murder

Lady Boynton is found dead on the outcrop. A stab wound to the stomach. A tiny needle prick on her neck. Wax around the wound that no one can explain.

Throughout the afternoon Lord Boynton kept asking different people to "go check on Mama" — and at various points each of them claims to have seen her alive. The timeline is impossible: Sarah's medical examination places death much earlier than the family's sightings.

The misdirections

  1. The syringe missing from Dr. Gerard's tent — pointing at the family, who all had access.
  2. Each family member claims to have seen her alive at different times — pointing at one of them lying.
  3. The nun side plot — Sister Agnieszka tries to abduct Jinny, turns out to be a white-slaver agent, gets killed. A complete red herring for the murder.
  4. Jefferson Cope's tenuous presence — looks suspicious, isn't relevant.

The trick

Each stepchild went up to the outcrop, saw Lady Boynton was already dead, assumed another sibling did it, and lied to protect them. So all the "alive" sightings are false — she was killed much earlier than anyone admitted. This is why Sarah's medical timeline didn't match the family's accounts.

The reveal

Westholme, disguised as an Arab servant, climbed to the outcrop and killed Lady Boynton early in the day — well before the family started their parade of denial. The wax on the wound is the giveaway: a candle was used in the method. The needle prick supports Poirot's reconstruction of how the digitalis-style poisoning was attempted alongside the stab.

The motive

Westholme was once a prisoner where Lady Boynton was the warden. Lady Boynton recognised her in Jerusalem — that "never forget a face" line was the warning shot. Westholme is now a celebrated public figure. Exposure of her criminal past would destroy her. So she killed first.

The denouement

Westholme overhears Poirot's drawing-room reconstruction from an adjoining room and shoots herself rather than be returned to prison.

Coda

The stepchildren are freed — Sarah and Raymond pair off, Carol pairs off, Jinny recovers. Leonard's fraud over the "John the Baptist skull" is gently exposed. The Nanny's role and the family's deeper trauma are wound up.

The structural pattern

The plot is built like a magic trick. Three "obvious" suspect pools are shown to the audience — the abused stepchildren, the suspicious outsiders (Cope, the nun), the convenient outsider (Sarah who confronted her). Each gets ruled out. The killer comes from the one place no one was looking: a respected public figure with a hidden past identity, recognised by the victim alone.

This is the same trick as Murder in Mesopotamia: Dr. Leidner is secretly Louise's presumed-dead first husband. Different mechanism, same sleight of hand — the killer is the one person whose connection to the victim's past is invisible to everyone else.
