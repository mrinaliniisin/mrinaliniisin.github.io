The plot, walked through

Opening — Europe on edge

It is the late 1930s. Europe is sliding toward war. A peace movement is rallying public opinion against rearmament. Into this anxious atmosphere arrives a series of strange, headline-grabbing murders that the press immediately attributes to a shadowy global criminal syndicate known as The Big Four: a Chinese mastermind (Number One — Li Chang Yen), an American tycoon (Number Two — Abe Ryland), a French scientist (Number Three — Madame Olivier), and a faceless killer-of-many-disguises (Number Four — "The Destroyer," supposedly an English actor named Claud Darrell).

The murders

  1. The Russian chess grandmaster Savaronoff is electrocuted at the board during a high-profile match — apparently by a poisoned chess piece.
  2. Stephen Paynter, an old China hand, is found stabbed in his study, with the words "YELLOW JASMINE" written in his own blood.
  3. The fading actress Flossie Monro, who had once known Claud Darrell, is strangled just as she prepares to identify him to Poirot.
  4. Madame Olivier, the famous French scientist, is murdered — neatly closing the loop on a "member of the Big Four being killed by her own organization" narrative.

Each murder leaves a calling card pointing at the Big Four. The press laps it up. Public fear escalates. The peace movement gains support: only by uniting can the world powers stand against this borderless menace.

Poirot's "death"

Poirot fakes his own death in an explosion to draw the killers out — and to summon Captain Hastings home from his ranch in Argentina. Hastings, Japp, and Miss Lemon, all believing the news, react with grief; the deception is cruel but operationally necessary. From hiding, Poirot watches who steps into the vacuum.

The misdirections

  1. Li Chang Yen — a name on a wall, a portrait, a set of rumours. Almost certainly a real but minor figure whose silhouette has been borrowed.
  2. Abe Ryland — a real American industrialist, peripherally connected to one of the murder scenes. Innocent of any conspiracy.
  3. Madame Olivier — the real, brilliant scientist whose supposed membership of the Big Four was useful to the hoaxers, and whose murder was useful to it too.
  4. Claud Darrell — the supposed master-of-disguise "Number Four." A fiction. There is no Claud Darrell.
  5. The journalist Tysoe — always present, always taking notes, always the first to file the next "Big Four" story. Looks like an obsessed reporter chasing the scoop. Is in fact the architect of the entire thing.

The trick

There is no Big Four. There never was. The conspiracy is an elaborate fiction assembled by Tysoe (the journalist) and Albert Whalley (a struggling, embittered actor) — Whalley playing the various disguised "Number Four" appearances and physically committing the killings, Tysoe writing the narrative and feeding it to the public. Real people (Li Chang Yen, Ryland, Olivier) were quietly cast in roles in a story they did not know they were part of.

The reveal

Returned from the dead, Poirot stages a drawing-room reconstruction. The murders, examined individually, do not point at a global syndicate at all — they point at one author and one performer. Tysoe is present in some role at every scene; Whalley's voice and gait can be detected under the disguises if one listens for them. The "yellow jasmine" message at Paynter's was a literary flourish from Tysoe's own published prose.

The motive

Tysoe is a true believer in peace. He is convinced that the only way to stop the coming war is to terrify the world powers into uniting against a common enemy — and if no such enemy exists, one must be invented. Murder in the service of preventing a far greater slaughter. Whalley, his accomplice, is in it partly for ideology, partly for the chance to play, finally, the role of his life.

The denouement

Tysoe and Whalley are taken. The "Big Four" headlines are quietly withdrawn. The peace movement loses its bogeyman. Hastings returns to Argentina, Japp and Miss Lemon back to their routines, Poirot to his flat. The war comes anyway.

The structural pattern

Where Appointment with Death, Murder in Mesopotamia, and Elephants Can Remember each turn on a single hidden identity (the killer's, or the corpse's), The Big Four turns on a hidden shape. The audience is shown a vast, geometric conspiracy — four masterminds, a global reach, calling cards, codenames — and the truth is that the geometry is a story, drawn around a tiny ground truth of two ordinary murderers with a private agenda.

The trick is the same family of move from a different angle: the apparent scale and shape of the case is the misdirection. Strip the costume off the conspiracy and what is left is a journalist and an actor.
