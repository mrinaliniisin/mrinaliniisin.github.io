The plot, walked through

Opening — Tell Yarimjah

An American-funded archaeological expedition is digging in northern Iraq, led by the gentle, scholarly Dr. Eric Leidner. His beautiful second wife Louise has joined him on site for the season. The atmosphere in the expedition house is poisonous: jealousies, infatuations, addictions, and Louise herself — magnetic, unsettling, and convinced someone is trying to kill her.

Nurse Amy Leatheran is hired as Louise's companion. She is the audience's eyes. Louise tells her she has been receiving threatening letters — letters that, she claims, started arriving years ago, then stopped, and have now begun again.

The letters

Louise's first husband was Frederick Bosner, a young man unmasked as a German spy during the First World War. He was supposedly killed in a train wreck while being transported. But ever since, whenever Louise has looked at marrying again, threatening letters have arrived in his handwriting: "You belong to me. If you marry another, you will die." Two earlier suitors were warned off. Then she married Eric Leidner — and the letters stopped. Now they have started again.

The murder

One hot afternoon, while everyone on the dig is accounted for in the courtyard or out in the field, Louise is found dead in her bedroom. She has been struck on the head with a heavy blunt object. The room opens onto a courtyard that was under more or less constant observation. No stranger could have entered. The killer must be one of the household.

The misdirections

  1. Father Lavigny, the visiting French monk and epigraphist, is behaving oddly — slipping out at night, his Arabic suspiciously poor for a scholar. He is in fact Raoul Menier, an antiquities thief in disguise, swapping genuine finds for clever fakes. A real criminal — but not Louise's killer.
  2. Richard Carey, the architect, was having an affair with Louise. Plenty of motive, plenty of guilt.
  3. Marie Mercado is jealous of Louise; her husband Joseph is a morphine addict whom Louise has been quietly threatening to expose.
  4. Bill Coleman has the same flamboyant handwriting as the threatening letters — and admits to having forged one of them as a stupid joke. Not all of them. Just one.
  5. Anne Johnson, the senior assistant, is silently in love with Leidner and resents Louise.

The second death

Anne Johnson works it out. Late one night she gasps to Nurse Leatheran: "I've seen how someone could have come in from outside — and yet not from outside." Before she can name the killer she is poisoned with hydrochloric acid swapped into her water glass. As she dies she manages one word: "window."

The trick

The murderer was inside Louise's room when she was killed, and never had to enter from the courtyard at all. Louise was struck through the open window from the flat roof above — but the simpler, deeper trick is the identity question. The threatening letters were not posted from outside. Most of them were written by the killer himself, planted in the household, and timed to make Louise terrified of a phantom from her past.

The reveal

Dr. Eric Leidner is Frederick Bosner. He survived the train wreck, returned years later under a new identity as a respected archaeologist, and courted and married his own former wife — who never recognised him. He had the perfect access, the perfect cover, and the perfect motive when Louise — who had begun to suspect — became dangerous to him. He killed her himself, in the room, and used the letters and the window to conceal both the method and the identity. Anne Johnson realised the truth and had to be silenced.

The motive

Possessive, obsessive love that curdled into the original threatening letters years before. "You belong to me. If you marry another, you will die." When Louise — even married to him under his new face — began to drift toward someone else, and worse, began to sense who he really was, the original threat had to be carried out.

The denouement

Poirot reconstructs the case in the dining room of the expedition house. Leidner does not deny it. The Lavigny / Menier antiquities-fraud side plot is wound up separately by Captain Maitland's men. Nurse Leatheran returns to England carrying the story.

The structural pattern

Three "obvious" suspect pools are paraded for the audience — the jealous wife and the addict husband, the secret lover Carey, the suspicious foreign monk. Each gets ruled out. The killer comes from the one place no one was looking: the gentle, devoted husband whose connection to the victim's past is invisible to everyone, including the victim herself.

This is the same trick as Appointment with Death: Dame Westholme is the prisoner Lady Boynton once held; Dr. Leidner is the first husband Louise long since buried. Different mechanism, same sleight of hand — the killer is the one person whose connection to the victim's past is invisible to everyone else.
