The plot, walked through

Opening — a literary lunch in London

Mystery novelist Mrs. Ariadne Oliver is cornered by an aggressive society woman, Mrs. Burton-Cox, who demands to know: "Did your goddaughter Celia's mother kill her father — or did her father kill her mother?" Mrs. Burton-Cox's son Desmond wants to marry Celia, and Mrs. Burton-Cox claims she has to know before she'll consent. Mrs. Oliver, appalled, takes the question to Poirot.

The cold case — 13 years ago at Overcliffe

General Sir Alistair Ravenscroft and his wife Lady Margaret Ravenscroft were found shot dead at the cliff edge of their Cornish home, Overcliffe. One pistol, both their fingerprints on it, both bullets fired from it. Verdict: murder-suicide. But which way round? No one knew, and the case was quietly closed. The two children, Celia and Edward, were sent away to relatives. The French governess, Mademoiselle Zelie Rouxelle, returned to Switzerland and never spoke of it.

Just before the deaths, Lady Margaret's twin sister, Dolly Jarrow, had come to live with them. Dolly had spent years institutionalised under the care of Dr. Willoughby Sr. — a child had died in her care many years before, and her mind had never recovered.

The present-day mystery

Just as Poirot and Mrs. Oliver begin to dig — interviewing the "elephants," the old witnesses who remember — the psychiatrist Dr. Willoughby (son of the man who treated Dolly) is murdered. Someone is killing the keepers of the old institution's records. Superintendent Garroway works the present-day case in parallel.

The misdirections

  1. Mrs. Burton-Cox's intrusive question looks like meddling — actually she has her own concealed motive tied to Desmond's inheritance and parentage.
  2. The murder-suicide verdict itself is misdirection — the "obvious" question (which spouse killed the other) is the wrong question. The right question is which woman is in the grave.
  3. The present-day Willoughby murders look connected to the cold case — they are connected by setting (the old institution) but the killer is a third party with a private grievance.
  4. The "elephants" — old servants, neighbours, the wig-maker, the postman — each remember a fragment, none of which on its own points to the truth. They are all telling Poirot what they saw; what matters is what they didn't see.

The trick

Lady Margaret had been killed weeks before the clifftop "murder-suicide." Dolly, her identical twin, had pushed Margaret to her death out of obsessive jealousy of the life Margaret had — a husband, children, sanity. Sir Alistair, with Zelie's help, quietly buried Margaret and then lived alongside Dolly as if she were Margaret, to protect the children from the scandal of their mother's murder by their aunt.

When the strain became unbearable — and when Dolly began to threaten the children too — Alistair took her up to the clifftop, shot her, and then shot himself. The body identified as "Lady Margaret" was Dolly. Margaret had been dead for weeks. The "murder-suicide" the world saw was real — but the woman in it was not who everyone thought.

The reveal

Zelie Rouxelle has carried the secret for thirteen years, out of love for Sir Alistair and loyalty to the children. Poirot extracts it from her gently. The wigs the old wig-maker remembers, the missing weeks, the small inconsistencies in the elephants' stories — all of it falls into place once you accept that Margaret died first and Dolly took her place.

The parallel resolution

The present-day Willoughby killings are revealed to be the work of Marie McDermott, who blames the Willoughby family — father and son — for the death of her own child years before, while she was a patient at the institution. Her grievance has nothing to do with the Ravenscrofts directly. The two mysteries shared a setting and a set of records, nothing more.

The motive

Dolly's motive: pathological envy of her twin's life. Sir Alistair's motive: protecting his children from a scandal worse than their parents' death — that their aunt had murdered their mother and lived in her skin. Marie McDermott's motive: long-incubated revenge against the doctors she blames for her child.

The denouement

Celia and Desmond can marry — knowing now that Lady Margaret was the victim, not the killer, and that Sir Alistair acted out of love for them. Mrs. Burton-Cox's leverage collapses. Marie McDermott is arrested. Mrs. Oliver, who started the whole thing, gets the last word.

The structural pattern

Two "obvious" suspect pools are paraded for the audience — the murder-suicide spouses (one of whom must be the killer), and the family of the present-day victim. The truth comes from the one place no one was looking: a third woman, identical to the victim, who has been hiding in plain sight as the victim herself.

This is the same family of trick as Appointment with Death (Westholme is the prisoner Lady Boynton once held) and Murder in Mesopotamia (Dr. Leidner is Louise's presumed-dead first husband). All three turn on a hidden identity that connects the killer or the victim to a buried past — the killer, or the corpse, is the one person whose true identity is invisible to everyone else.
